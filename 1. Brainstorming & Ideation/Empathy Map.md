## 2. Empathy Map

> **Empathy Map Structure:** The classic 4-quadrant empathy map (Says / Thinks / Does / Feels) is expanded with Pains and Gains sections to provide a full 360° view of the user experience.

---

### 2.1 🗣️ SAYS
*What does Sumiya literally say out loud — to friends, at events, in conversations?*

**Before the Event:**
- *"I really want to talk to the hiring manager from OpenAI, but I don't know what to say."*
- *"I'll just walk around and see who I bump into, I guess."*
- *"Should I research the companies beforehand? I don't know where to start."*
- *"My friend says just be yourself, but that hasn't been working."*
- *"I practiced my elevator pitch but I always forget it when the moment comes."*

**During the Event (attempted openers):**
- *"Hi, um… so what team are you on?"*
- *"I really like your company's work on… the machine learning stuff."*
- *"I read a bit about your product, it's really interesting."*
- *"So, have you been to this conference before?"* *(said for lack of anything better)*
- *"I'm a grad student, focusing on NLP. I hope to work in AI someday."* *(feels too scripted)*

**After the Event:**
- *"I barely talked to anyone who could actually help me."*
- *"I got a few business cards but I don't know how to follow up."*
- *"The person from Google was right there and I just froze."*
- *"I need to be better prepared next time."*

---

### 2.2 💭 THINKS
*What is going through Sumiya's mind — internal monologue, anxieties, assumptions she doesn't say aloud?*

**About Herself:**
- *"Am I interesting enough to approach someone senior?"*
- *"What if they think my question is dumb or irrelevant?"*
- *"I'm an international student — will they take me seriously?"*
- *"I don't have impressive internship experience yet. What do I even offer in this conversation?"*
- *"Everyone else seems to know what to say. Why is this so hard for me?"*

**About the Event:**
- *"I don't know enough about this conference topic to say anything smart."*
- *"There are 200 people here and I've talked to 3."*
- *"That speaker mentioned something about transformer models — should I ask about that? But what exactly would I ask?"*
- *"The clock is ticking and I'm spending time at the food table."*

**About the Future:**
- *"If I don't network better, I won't find a good internship."*
- *"What if that person would have been a perfect mentor but I missed the chance?"*
- *"I need a system. I can't keep winging this."*
- *"If only I had something smart and specific to open with, I could take it from there."*

---

### 2.3 🏃 DOES
*What are Sumiya's observable actions and behaviors before, during, and after events?*

**Weeks Before the Event:**
- Registers for the event on Eventbrite / university portal
- Marks it in her calendar but doesn't immediately prepare
- Googles the event name once to skim the schedule
- Spends more time on coursework than event prep

**Days Before the Event:**
- Checks the speaker list and reads 1–2 LinkedIn profiles briefly
- Tries to draft possible conversation starters in her notes app — abandons after 3 attempts
- Asks her roommate: *"What would you say to a Google recruiter?"*
- Picks an outfit strategically — wants to appear professional yet approachable

**Day of the Event:**
- Arrives on time but stands near the entrance observing others for 10–15 minutes
- Gravitates toward people she already knows (classmates, professors)
- Approaches a recruiter once — gets flustered after her elevator pitch ends and the recruiter asks a follow-up question
- Collects 3 business cards by being approached by others, not by initiating
- Spends 20 minutes by the refreshments table "strategizing"

**After the Event:**
- Sends one generic LinkedIn connection request without a note
- Writes in her journal: *"I need to do better."*
- Searches YouTube for "how to network at conferences" — watches 2 videos but finds advice too generic
- Sets a reminder to "practice networking" — reminder gets ignored

## 3. PAINS
*Specific, concrete frustrations, obstacles, and risks Sumiya experiences*

### 3.1 Pain Points in Detail

#### 🔴 Pain 1: Cognitive Freeze at the Crucial Moment
**Description:** Even when Sumiya has prepared an opener in her head, the presence of a stranger causes her to forget it entirely. The social pressure of "performing" collapses her pre-planned script.
**Trigger:** Making eye contact with a senior professional or recruiter
**Consequence:** Defaults to the most generic opener available; conversation stalls quickly
**Frequency:** Every networking event, with every new contact
**Severity:** High — directly prevents meaningful connection

---

#### 🔴 Pain 2: No Factual Knowledge to Anchor Conversation
**Description:** Sumiya wants to ask an insightful question (e.g., about a company's recent product release or a conference speaker's research), but she doesn't have that knowledge readily available.
**Trigger:** Wanting to sound informed about the event topic or the person's work
**Consequence:** Conversations stay shallow; she cannot pivot from small talk to substantive discussion
**Frequency:** Every event, particularly in specialized domains (fintech, biotech, cloud infra)
**Severity:** High — directly limits conversation depth

---

#### 🟠 Pain 3: Generic Networking Advice Doesn't Help
**Description:** Sumiya has consumed substantial advice ("be curious," "ask open-ended questions," "find common ground") but none of it translates to a specific, usable sentence she can say.
**Trigger:** Researching networking tips the night before an event
**Consequence:** Feeling more aware of the problem without being any better equipped to solve it
**Frequency:** After every event, in the prep cycle
**Severity:** Medium — creates frustration without resolution

---

#### 🟠 Pain 4: Fear of Misrepresenting Herself
**Description:** Sumiya is afraid of confidently stating a fact about a technology or company that turns out to be wrong, which she perceives would permanently damage her credibility.
**Trigger:** Mentioning a product feature, research result, or industry trend she's not 100% sure about
**Consequence:** Self-censorship; avoids substantive topics to avoid risk of error
**Frequency:** Frequently, especially in technically dense events
**Severity:** Medium-High — leads to surface-level conversation

---

#### 🟡 Pain 5: Post-Event Follow-Up Paralysis
**Description:** After collecting business cards, Sumiya doesn't know what to write in a LinkedIn message or email. Without a strong event-specific shared topic, her follow-up feels generic and forgettable.
**Trigger:** Sitting down to write LinkedIn connection notes after an event
**Consequence:** Sends no messages or sends generic ones that are rarely replied to
**Frequency:** After every event
**Severity:** Medium — reduces the long-term value of attending events

## 4. GAINS
*What Sumiya hopes to achieve, what success looks, feels, and sounds like*

### 4.1 Desired Outcomes

#### ✅ Gain 1: Confidence to Approach Anyone
**What success looks like:** Sumiya walks up to a Google engineering manager and opens with: *"I noticed you spoke about using LLMs for code review — I'm working on something similar in my thesis. Could I ask you how you handle factual grounding in generated outputs?"*
**Why it matters:** Specific, confident, and relevant — immediately establishes her as a serious professional
**How the app helps:** GPT-2 generates this exact type of event-specific, person-aware opener based on her inputs

---

#### ✅ Gain 2: Factually Grounded Content She Can Trust
**What success looks like:** The app shows: *"Wikipedia says: 'Google DeepMind's AlphaCode 2 outperformed 85% of competition participants…'"* embedded into a starter she can use
**Why it matters:** She can cite a real fact confidently, reducing risk of error
**How the app helps:** Wikipedia API fetches verified, sourced content that is displayed transparently alongside each starter

---

#### ✅ Gain 3: A System for Event Prep (Not Just More Advice)
**What success looks like:** 30 minutes before an event, Sumiya opens the web app, fills in: Event = "AI Ethics Summit", Person = "a machine learning researcher from a policy NGO", Tone = "Curious", and gets 4 ready-to-use openers
**Why it matters:** Replaces anxiety with action; gives her a process to follow
**How the app helps:** Streamlit UI provides a structured, fast workflow from input to output

---

#### ✅ Gain 4: Tone-Matched Communication
**What success looks like:** At a formal academic conference, Sumiya selects "Professional" tone and gets starters that sound polished. At a casual startup networking night, she selects "Friendly" and gets openers that feel natural
**Why it matters:** Different events require different registers; tone mismatch is noticeable and off-putting
**How the app helps:** Tone selector modulates the GPT-2 generation prompt accordingly

---

#### ✅ Gain 5: Better Follow-Up Conversations
**What success looks like:** Because her event conversations were more substantive, Sumiya has a real shared topic to reference in her LinkedIn message: *"It was great discussing transformer interpretability with you at the AI Summit — I'm curious about your paper on attention visualization."*
**Why it matters:** Strong follow-ups lead to mentor relationships, referrals, and job opportunities
**How the app helps:** By improving the quality of the conversation, the app indirectly improves every downstream networking outcome

## 5. How the App Addresses Each Empathy Map Section

| Empathy Section | User Need | App Response |
|---|---|---|
| **SAYS** (generic openers) | Replace "What do you do?" with event-specific starters | GPT-2 generates contextually relevant, non-generic openers |
| **THINKS** (impostor syndrome) | Reduce anxiety by giving prepared, smart content | Pre-generated starters reduce cognitive load in the moment |
| **DOES** (underprepared event arrival) | Provide a 5-minute prep workflow | Streamlit form: fill → submit → copy starter → go |
| **FEELS** (anxious, deflated) | Shift emotional state from anxious to prepared | Structured tool replaces formless worry with actionable output |
| **PAINS** (cognitive freeze) | Provide ready-to-use scripts, not just advice | 3–5 starters ready to copy-paste or memorize |
| **PAINS** (no factual knowledge) | Surface relevant facts automatically | Wikipedia API fetches event-topic facts without user research |
| **PAINS** (fear of inaccuracy) | Ground AI output in real-world facts | Wikipedia snippet shown with source; user can verify |
| **GAINS** (confidence) | Build confidence through preparation | Having a smart opener prepared reduces social anxiety |
| **GAINS** (trust in content) | Ensure outputs are factually credible | Wikipedia-enriched prompts anchor GPT-2 output in reality |
| **GAINS** (systematic prep) | Replace vague advice with a process | App provides a repeatable, structured workflow |

## 6. Design Implications from Empathy Map

Based on the empathy map insights, the following design decisions were made:

| Insight | Design Decision |
|---|---|
| Sumiya freezes under pressure — needs the starter *before* the event | App is a **preparation tool**, not a real-time assistant |
| She doesn't trust herself to know enough about event topics | Wikipedia enrichment is **always on** and results are **shown prominently** |
| She wants to sound natural, not robotic | GPT-2 output is post-processed to avoid overly formal or template-sounding phrases |
| She doesn't have time for complex workflows | Streamlit form has **4 inputs max** — event name, event type (auto-classified), person description, tone |
| She wants multiple options | App generates **3–5 starters** (not 1) so she can choose the one that feels most natural |
| She's a graduate student on a budget | App uses **free, open-source models** — no API keys or paid subscriptions required |
| She may be unfamiliar with AI model limitations | Wikipedia source snippet is shown **with the output** to set expectations about factual grounding |
